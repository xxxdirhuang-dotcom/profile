From Childhood Memory to East Asian Emotional Expression

Performing Lin Hai‑yin’s Memories of Peking as an Intercultural Solo — examining childhood, East Asian family dynamics, adaptation, and the aesthetics of rain, oleander, and farewell song.

About the Artist

I am a Chinese actor and maker based in the UK. This website documents my practice‑as‑research project My Father’s Flowers Have Fallen, a seven‑minute solo adaptation drawn from Lin Hai‑yin’s Memories of Peking. The work investigates how an Eastern childhood narrative can communicate across cultures through an English‑language solo performance.

About the Performance

My Father’s Flowers Have Fallen centres on Yingzi, a 12‑year‑old girl in 1920s Beijing. Told in English for a UK audience, the piece blends first‑person narration with enacted memory. Rain soundscapes cue temporal shifts; the oleander marks a tender yet painful filial bond; a graduation folk song threads the arc of growing‑up and letting‑go.

The performance balances cultural specificity with accessibility. It embraces Linda Hutcheon’s view of adaptation as “repetition without replication,” treating the solo as a new work in dialogue with the novel.

Creative Notes

  • Voice: Adult retrospection layered over a child’s immediacy
  • Form: Jump‑in/jump‑out solo — multiple roles, narrator shifts
  • Design: Minimal bench; hand‑held flower; rain SFX; live humming of the farewell song

Research Background

Intercultural & Adaptation

Guided by Erika Fischer‑Lichte and Patrice Pavis on intercultural performance, and by Linda Hutcheon on adaptation, the project negotiates ethics and efficacy: honour the source while crafting a standalone, culturally legible performance.

Diaspora Parallels

Lin Yutang and Ha Jin offer precedents for telling Chinese stories in English — balancing fidelity to cultural sensibility with clarity for international audiences.

Practice-as-Research

I reflect on doubt, time‑limits, and depth: fear of “only playing a child” versus achieving a layered adult‑looking‑back perspective. Embracing uncertainty (after Robin Nelson), I prioritised sincerity over perfection. Audience responses suggested the core emotion resonated despite brevity.
